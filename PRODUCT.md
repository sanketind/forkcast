## Why Restaurants Fail — The Real Problem Hierarchy

Running a restaurant is not the easiest business to start. It is one of the hardest.

60–70% of new restaurants in India close within the first year. The food is usually not the problem. The numbers are. Owners price dishes by looking at what the place next door charges, not by calculating their own cost. They order ingredients by memory. They hire staff based on yesterday's rush. They confuse revenue with profit — sometimes until the bank account runs dry.

**The problems, in order of how quickly they kill a restaurant:**

### P1 — Financial Blindness *(kills in 3–12 months, silently)*

The owner does not know if the business is profitable until cash runs out.

- **No break-even number** — Most owners know monthly rent (₹60,000) but have never translated that into "I need ₹15,878 per day before I've covered a single rupee of cost." When fixed costs and variable costs are combined, the real break-even is usually 2× what the owner assumes.
- **Food cost tracked by feel** — When onion goes from ₹20/kg to ₹80/kg and the menu price doesn't change, food cost climbs from 30% to 48% silently — over weeks. The owner notices only at month-end when there is less cash than expected.
- **Revenue ≠ profit** — ₹8L/month revenue with 42% food cost, 33% labour, 10% rent, and 8% overheads leaves ₹56,000 net profit. Many owners feel successful doing ₹8L and do not know this number. A bad month at ₹6L with the same cost structure means a loss.
- **Dish pricing by competition, not cost** — "I charge ₹180 because the place next door charges ₹200." But their actual food cost per plate is ₹75 (42%). The margin is negative before overheads.

### P2 — Demand Guessing *(costs money every single day)*

Every morning: how much to order, how many staff to call in, what to prep. All done on gut feel.

- Ingredient ordering by memory → 20–30% food waste industry-wide from over-ordering, or emergency premium-price purchases from under-ordering
- Staffing by yesterday's feel → ₹2,000–₹4,000 in wasted wages on slow days, or a chaotic dinner rush with two cooks and six tables waiting
- No calendar awareness → ordered a full week of chicken during Shravan month, not knowing non-veg demand drops 50%. ₹18,000 of waste.
- No festival foresight → three cooks scheduled for New Year's Eve. ₹60,000 of business walked away because the kitchen couldn't handle it.

### P3 — Supply Blindness *(infrequent, but catastrophic when it hits)*

The owner is always the last to know when supply breaks.

- LPG shortage → 3-day closure during a busy weekend. Zero warning, zero time to source alternative.
- Onion crisis (2021) → ₹20/kg to ₹80/kg in two weeks. No adjustment to ordering, no adjustment to menu pricing. Food cost for that month: 51%.
- Cooking oil disruption (2022) → landed cost doubled. No alert, no buffer stock, no menu price change.

### P4 — Menu Stagnation *(slow margin erosion over months)*

The menu doesn't change. Costs do.

- Best-selling dish is a Plow Horse — high orders, low margin. It drives revenue but eats profit.
- Costs go up 8–15% per year, menu prices stay flat for 2–3 years. Margin erodes silently.
- No data on which dishes actually make money versus which are just popular.

## The Origin Insight 

A restaurant owner in Pune had to close for three days because LPG cylinders ran out during a geopolitical supply disruption. He had a perfect demand forecast — he knew it would be a busy weekend. But it didn't matter. The gas ran out.

The information that the shortage was coming was publicly available — news headlines, mandi prices, social chatter. But there was no system watching for it and connecting it to his specific kitchen.

This is why Forkcast tracks supply signals alongside demand. Knowing you'll do ₹35,000 tomorrow is useless if you can't cook. **The forecast is the foundation. The supply watch is the insurance.**

### 3. Unique Value Proposition

> **"₹31,400 tomorrow. After costs: ₹3,200 profit. Here's what to change."**

The primary value is not just knowing tomorrow's revenue — it's knowing whether the business is actually working. Revenue without cost context is vanity. Forkcast is the only tool that connects the daily forecast to the daily P&L so an owner can see: *"I'm forecasting ₹31,400, my break-even is ₹28,000 — I'm fine. But my food cost is at 41% this week. Every restaurant analytics tool shows what happened yesterday. Forkcast shows what will happen tomorrow — and whether it's profitable. The combination of forecast + cost tracking + India-specific signals turns it from a nice-to-have into a business-critical tool. When an owner sees food cost creeping from 32% to 40% over six weeks, they'll pay any subscription price for the tool that caught it.

## Restaurant Business Health — The Numbers That Actually Matter

*This is what separates a restaurant that survives from one that doesn't. Most owners don't track any of this. Forkcast should surface all of it.*

---

### The Industry P&L Structure

A restaurant's revenue gets consumed in this order. What's left is the owner's income.

```
Gross Revenue (100%)
  − Food Cost            (target: 28–35%)
  − Labour Cost          (target: 25–35%)
                         ─────────────────
  = Gross Profit         (target: 35–45%)
  − Rent                 (target: <10%, ideally <8%)
  − Utilities            (electricity, water, gas: 3–5%)
  − Packaging            (delivery-heavy: 2–4%)
  − Licences & insurance (1–2%)
  − Maintenance & misc   (1–2%)
                         ─────────────────
  = Net Profit           (industry average: 3–9%)
                         (excellent: 12–18%)
```

**The hard truth:** A restaurant doing ₹8L/month with 38% food cost, 32% labour, 10% rent, and 8% overheads makes ₹96,000/month net — 12%. That's good. But if food cost climbs to 45% during a price spike and menu prices don't change, net profit drops to ₹40,000. The owner doesn't feel it immediately. It shows up as "less cash" three weeks later. By then, the month is gone.

---

### Key Metrics Every Restaurant Owner Must Track

#### Daily (Forkcast computes from POS data)


| Metric                        | Formula                      | Healthy range                       | What it tells you                     |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| **Daily revenue**             | Sum of all sales             | Benchmark against break-even        | Are you covering costs today?         |
| **Average Order Value (AOV)** | Revenue ÷ Orders             | ₹200–₹600 (QSR–casual)              | Is upselling working?                 |
| **Revenue per cover**         | Revenue ÷ Covers served      | Trend over time                     | Is spend per person going up or down? |
| **Table turnover rate**       | Covers ÷ Seats               | Lunch: 2–3×, Dinner: 1.5–2×         | Are tables sitting empty too long?    |
| **Dish contribution**         | Orders × (price − food cost) | Top 3 dishes → 50%+ of total margin | What's actually making you money?     |


#### Weekly


| Metric                  | Formula                                 | Healthy range         | Red flag                             |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| **Food cost %**         | Ingredient spend ÷ Revenue × 100        | 28–35%                | >40% = margin crisis                 |
| **Labour cost %**       | Staff wages ÷ Revenue × 100             | 25–35%                | >38% = over-staffed or under-revenue |
| **Prime cost**          | Food cost % + Labour cost %             | 55–65%                | >68% = business in trouble           |
| **Waste %**             | Wasted ingredients ÷ Total purchased    | <5%                   | >10% = over-ordering or poor prep    |
| **Break-even progress** | Cumulative revenue ÷ Monthly break-even | On track by day 18–22 | Break-even after day 25 = loss month |


#### Monthly


| Metric                    | Formula                          | Healthy range     | Why it matters                                              |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------------- | ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Net profit margin**     | Net profit ÷ Revenue × 100       | 5–12%             | The real health number                                      |
| **Rent-to-revenue ratio** | Monthly rent ÷ Revenue × 100     | <10%              | >15% = location too expensive for volume                    |
| **Revenue per sq ft**     | Revenue ÷ Carpet area            | ₹800–₹2,500/sq ft | Is the space earning its cost?                              |
| **Repeat customer rate**  | Returning customers ÷ Total      | >40%              | Below 25% = quality or value problem                        |
| **Online order mix**      | Delivery revenue ÷ Total revenue | 20–60% (varies)   | High delivery = packaging + 18–25% commission eating margin |

### The Break-Even Calculator — Forkcast's Most Important Panel (Planned)

**The single most useful number a restaurant owner can know is their daily break-even.**

Most owners have never calculated it. Here is the formula with a real example:

```
Monthly Fixed Costs:
  Rent:               ₹60,000
  Permanent staff:    ₹1,20,000
  Electricity:        ₹15,000
  Gas (LPG/PNG):      ₹12,000
  Internet + POS:     ₹3,000
  Licences:           ₹5,000
  EMI / loan:         ₹20,000
  ──────────────────────────────
  Total fixed:        ₹2,35,000

Variable cost per rupee of revenue:
  Food cost:          35%
  Packaging:          3%
  Variable labour:    5%
  ──────────────────────────────
  Variable cost %:    43%

Daily break-even:
  = Fixed cost ÷ (1 − variable %) ÷ Working days
  = ₹2,35,000 ÷ 0.57 ÷ 26
  = ₹15,878 / day

→ Every rupee above ₹15,878 is profit.
→ Every rupee below it is a loss day.
```

**What Forkcast does with this:**

- Owner enters fixed costs once in Settings (takes 5 minutes)
- Every morning: *"Forecast: ₹28,400. Break-even: ₹15,878. Projected profit contribution: ₹12,522."*
- Every evening: *"Actual: ₹24,100. Profit contribution: ₹8,222."*
- Monthly: *"You hit break-even on Day 14. Last month: Day 19. Improving."*

### Menu Engineering — Which Dishes to Keep, Price, or Drop

The restaurant industry uses a 2×2 matrix: **popularity** (order volume) × **profitability** (contribution margin = price − food cost).

```
                    HIGH MARGIN
                         │
    PUZZLES              │    STARS ⭐
    (high margin,        │    (high margin,
     low orders)         │     high orders)
    → Fix the dish,      │    → Feature prominently,
      reprice, or drop   │      never discount
─────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────
    DOGS                 │    PLOWHORSES
    (low margin,         │    (low margin,
     low orders)         │     high orders)
    → Eliminate unless   │    → Reprice upward or
      strategic reason   │      reduce portion cost
                         │
                    LOW MARGIN
```

**What Forkcast computes from POS + recipe data:**

- Popularity: order frequency from sales history
- Profitability: price − food cost per dish (from recipe mappings)
- Classification: Star / Plow Horse / Puzzle / Dog per dish
- Action: *"Butter Chicken is a Plow Horse — 240 orders/week but only 26% margin. Add ₹30 on weekends or switch to a cheaper cut."*

### Category 1: The Indian Religious & Fasting Calendar

This is the single most underestimated signal in Indian restaurant forecasting. Western tools have no concept of it. It is not optional — it is the difference between a 40% revenue drop and a normal day.

#### Recurring weekly fasting days


| Day                 | Tradition        | Non-veg impact | Veg impact | Cities most affected              |
| ------------------- | ---------------- | -------------- | ---------- | --------------------------------- |
| Monday              | Lord Shiva       | −10 to −20%    | +5 to +10% | All India                         |
| Tuesday             | Hanuman / Mangal | −10 to −15%    | +5%        | Maharashtra, UP, MP               |
| Thursday            | Guru / Vishnu    | −8 to −12%     | +5%        | Maharashtra, Gujarat, South India |
| Saturday            | Shani            | −10 to −15%    | +5%        | Maharashtra, North India          |
| Full Moon (Purnima) | Various          | −15 to −25%    | +10%       | All India                         |
| New Moon (Amavasya) | Pitru paksha     | −20 to −30%    | +8%        | All India                         |


**Ekadashi (twice monthly)** ✅ in engine — 11th day of each lunar fortnight. Non-veg demand drops 20–35%. Pure veg restaurants see +25–40%. Many communities avoid even onion and garlic — dish mix changes, not just volume.

#### Annual fasting periods


| Period                    | Duration    | When         | Non-veg impact       | Veg impact      | Region                                        |
| ------------------------- | ----------- | ------------ | -------------------- | --------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Shravan (Sawan)**       | 30–35 days  | July–August  | **−40 to −60%**      | **+30 to +50%** | All North & West India; Maharashtra           |
| **Navratri** (×2/year)    | 9 days each | Mar–Apr, Oct | **−35 to −50%**      | **+40 to +60%** | All India; strongest Gujarat, UP, Maharashtra |
| **Pitru Paksha (Shradh)** | 16 days     | Sept–Oct     | −25 to −35%          | +15%            | North India, Maharashtra                      |
| **Paryushana (Jain)**     | 8–10 days   | Aug–Sept     | −20% near Jain areas | +20 to +35%     | Pune, Mumbai, Ahmedabad, Surat                |
| **Margashirsha**          | 30 days     | Nov–Dec      | −10 to −20%          | +10%            | Maharashtra (Thursdays especially)            |
| **Ashadha**               | 30 days     | June–July    | −15 to −25%          | +10%            | Maharashtra                                   |
| **Kartik month**          | 30 days     | Oct–Nov      | −20 to −30%          | +15 to +20%     | North India                                   |


> **Shravan is the most important single signal for non-veg restaurants.** A biryani restaurant in Pune can see 50% revenue drop for an entire month. No analytics tool currently warns owners 30 days in advance to plan cash flow and ordering accordingly.
